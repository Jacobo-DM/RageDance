0. Introduction

RageDance is an embodied movement practice grounded in breath, rhythm, somatic awareness, cultural listening, and the transformation of difficult emotions into clarity and presence. It is simple enough to learn in minutes and deep enough to grow with for a lifetime.

RageDance rests on three basic commitments:

  • Stay in your body.
  • Feel honestly.
  • Move without harm.

This primer provides the core framework used across Flower Road, Discovery Mode sessions, youth workshops, and personal practice.

1. What RageDance Is

RageDance is:

  • A rhythmic movement practice built on somatic check-ins, breath, martial arts fundamentals, and cultural rhythm.
  • A small-space discipline (the 18-inch mat) requiring focus, control, and emotional honesty.
  • A trauma-aware practice: grounded, non-coercive, and invitational.
  • A way to metabolize anger, fear, grief, confusion, and adrenaline into coordinated movement and steady breath.
  • A bridge between individuals and communities: a shared pulse where emotions become movement rather than rupture.
  • A Flower Road practice dedicated to healing without spectacle.

2. What RageDance Is Not

RageDance is not:

  • A performance or show.
  • A fight choreography system.
  • A substitute for therapy or medical care.
  • A competitive form.
  • A spectacle designed for audiences.
  • A tool for domination, intimidation, or coercion.
  • A framework to be militarized, surveilled, or commercialized.

RageDance stays close to the earth, held by breath and choice.

3. The 18-Inch Mat Philosophy

RageDance is practiced inside a small square—approximately 18 inches by 18 inches. This constraint is intentional:

  • It promotes precision.
  • It helps prevent escalation.
  • It demands self-regulation.
  • It mirrors the emotional discipline required in real conflict.
  • It honors personal boundaries.
  • It allows practice in classrooms, studios, parks, and small shared spaces.

The mat is not a limit. It is a teacher.

4. The RageDance Cycle (Eight Phases)

The core RageDance cycle follows eight phases:

  1. Arrival & Breath
  2. Body Check-In
  3. Neck & Facial Tapping
  4. Shoulder Circles
  5. Qigong Sway + Latin Hip Integration
  6. Twin Infinity Loops (right & left)
  7. Elbow Arc Drills
  8. Pulse Reflection & Exit

Each phase can be taught in 2–3 minutes, extended to 10, or combined for a full session.

5. Phase 1 — Arrival & Breath

Purpose: anchor awareness, reset the nervous system, build internal warmth.

Instructions:

  • Stand centered in your 18-inch square.
  • Feet grounded, knees soft.
  • Inhale through the nose, exhale through the mouth.
  • Let the shoulders soften on each exhale.
  • Eyes relaxed or gently lowered.

Breath is the spine of RageDance. Every movement returns to it.

6. Phase 2 — Body Check-In

A foundational somatic ritual for children, teens, and adults. The goal is awareness, not judgment.

Move your attention from top to bottom:

  • Shoulders: soft or tight?
  • Chest: open or guarded?
  • Stomach: held or loose?
  • Hips: fluid or locked?
  • Legs: grounded or restless?
  • Feet: numb, tense, or alive?

This is not about “fixing.” It is about noticing. The body is always telling the truth; RageDance begins by listening.

7. Phase 3 — Neck, Cranial, & Facial Tapping

These techniques appear in many traditions and serve as a simple nervous system reset.

Neck:

  • Using thumb and fingers, gently massage the muscles along either side of the spine.
  • Trace upward into the base of the skull.

Cranial:

  • Tap lightly across the skull with open fingertips.

Face:

  • Tap gently along cheeks, forehead, jawline, and around the eyes.

Purpose:

  • Release hidden tension.
  • Help prevent neck and shoulder strain.
  • Wake up the nervous system in a gentle way.
  • Support emotional regulation.

8. Phase 4 — Shoulder Circles (Small to Large)

Shoulder circles are a bridge between warm-up and deeper work.

  • Begin with small, slow rotations of both shoulders.
  • Gradually increase the size of the circles.
  • Let the chest begin to open as the shoulders move.
  • Keep the jaw relaxed and breath steady.

The shoulders open the doors of the chest; the chest opens the doors of the heart.

Suggested music: Shaggy – “Feeling Good”

9. Phase 5 — Qigong Sway + Latin Hip Integration

Step 1: Qigong sway

  • Stand with feet about shoulder-width apart.
  • Let the arms swing forward and back in a relaxed manner.
  • Allow the weight to gently shift from foot to foot.

Step 2: Add Latin hips

  • Introduce a small figure-8 motion through the hips.
  • Keep the knees soft and ready to absorb movement.
  • Let the spine ripple gently with the rhythm.

Suggested music: Orishas – “Devórame”

This fusion awakens the whole body without forcing intensity. It is playful, grounded, and emotionally accessible.

10. Phase 6 — Twin Infinity Loops

Twin infinity loops are a signature RageDance pattern.

Right arm pattern:

  • From center, cross the body upward.
  • Draw a wide loop across the front of the body.
  • Rise through the midline and over the shoulder.
  • Return to center.

Left arm pattern:

  • Mirror the same pattern on the left side.

When both arms move in opposite phases, the body begins to organize around the loops. Shoulders, spine, hips, and legs instinctively adjust to keep balance and flow.

The loops can be practiced slowly for calm, or faster as a prelude to martial drills.

11. Phase 7 — Elbow Arc Drills

Elbow work is the martial root of RageDance, emphasizing precision, dignity, and boundaries.

Basic pattern (each side):

  • Start with the elbow close to the body.
  • Coil the shoulder and torso slightly.
  • Lift the elbow through a controlled arc upward.
  • Drop through a diagonal path, covering multiple angles.
  • Return to center with control.

Train both sides equally: several arcs on the left, then several on the right, then alternate. Breath leads power; precision outweighs speed.

12. Phase 8 — Pulse Reflection & Exit

RageDance ends with a return to stillness and a brief check-in.

Questions you can ask:

  • What is my pulse doing now?
  • Where is my breath?
  • What changed during this session?
  • What softened? What strengthened?

A practice is complete when you can answer with honesty, not perfection.

13. Music Architecture

Music in RageDance is used with intention, not as background noise.

  • To build grounding and cadence.
  • To help recognize emotional states.
  • To frame cultural context and street stories.
  • To support controlled escalation and recovery.

One guiding question is: “What is the pulse, and what story does it carry?”

14. Teaching Guidelines

RageDance is trauma-aware and culturally respectful. Teachers and facilitators agree to:

  • Keep participation voluntary; students may pause or stop at any time.
  • Avoid physically moving or manipulating participants into position.
  • Use language that is clear, calm, and non-shaming.
  • Acknowledge the cultural threads and lived experiences from which the practice arises.
  • Maintain the small-space discipline (the 18-inch mat) to support safety.
  • Encourage emotional literacy without demanding personal disclosure.
  • Normalize rest and pauses as part of the practice.

15. Safety Notes

  • Hydrate before and after practice.
  • Warm up neck, shoulders, and hips thoroughly.
  • Avoid abrupt, jerking movements or ballistic strikes.
  • Respect joint limits at all times.
  • Stop if dizziness, numbness, or sharp pain occurs.
  • With children and youth, emphasize “slow first, always.”

16. Sample 10–15 Minute Session

A compact RageDance session might look like this:

  1. Arrival & Breath (1 minute)
  2. Body Check-In (2 minutes)
  3. Neck & Facial Tapping (1 minute)
  4. Shoulder Circles (2 minutes)
  5. Qigong Sway + Latin Hips (2 minutes)
  6. Twin Infinity Loops (2 minutes)
  7. Elbow Arc Drills (2 minutes)
  8. Pulse Reflection & Exit (1 minute)
