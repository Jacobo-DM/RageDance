Sharing RageDance With Others

Guidelines for classrooms, youth spaces, and community settings.

A Flower Road Practice by JH Durbin with Elumannín · ← Back to RageDance Home

Before You Begin

RageDance is a somatic practice for moving strong feelings in a small space. When you bring it into a classroom or group setting, your job is not to perform or “lead” perfectly. Your job is to protect agency, safety, and dignity.

Key Principles

  • Voluntary: No one has to join. Offer alternatives like seated breath, drawing, or quiet swaying.
  • Non-coercive: Never single someone out for not participating. No shaming, no pressure.
  • Small-space aware: Keep movements contained and precise. This is not a flailing practice.
  • Trauma-aware: You do not know what people carry. Stay gentle, invitational, and observant.
Commons Reminder: RageDance (Flower Road Practice) is distinct from similarly-named commercial offerings and must remain in the non-commercial, trauma-aware, community-rooted commons.

A Simple 10–15 Minute Session Template

This template is designed for classrooms, youth centers, and small group spaces.

1. Framing (1 minute)

  • Explain briefly: “We’re going to do a short movement practice called RageDance. It’s here to help us move stress, anger, and tension safely.”
  • Say clearly: “You do not have to join. You can also sit, watch, or do a quieter version.”

2. Arrival & Breath (2 minutes)

  • Invite everyone to stand (or sit) in their spot.
  • Guide 5–8 slow breaths: in through the nose, out through the mouth.
  • Keep your own voice steady and calm.

3. Light Warm-Up (3–4 minutes)

  • Lead gentle neck tapping, shoulder rolls, and Qigong sway.
  • Model doing less, not more. Let students choose range and intensity.

4. Focused Movement (3–5 minutes)

  • Introduce 1–2 simple patterns: Twin Infinity Loops, or Elbow Arcs.
  • Offer this frame: “This isn’t a dance show. It’s just us moving what we’re holding.”

5. Exit & Reflection (2–3 minutes)

  • Invite everyone back to stillness or gentle sway.
  • Ask a simple closing question: “What feels a little different now?”
  • You can model a short answer like, “My shoulders feel lighter,” or “My breathing feels slower.”
Tip: End before the group is exhausted. RageDance should leave people feeling a little more awake, not drained.

Adaptation & Culture

RageDance is a feral garden practice. It should adapt to the culture, music, and stories of the people using it.

  • Invite students to suggest music that feels grounding or energizing (within your context’s limits).
  • Allow different styles of movement as long as they are safe and non-harming.
  • Make space for humor. Laughter is part of the medicine.

Your Role as Steward

In RageDance, you are not a “master” or guru. You are a steward. You keep the container simple, safe, and open.

  • Model consent: show that you adjust movements to your own limits.
  • Model humility: it’s okay to be a bit awkward or unsure.
  • Model care: notice if someone looks distressed and quietly give them options.

Boundaries & Red Lines

  • Do not use RageDance as discipline or punishment.
  • Do not force anyone to share what they felt.
  • Do not film participants without explicit, informed consent (and follow local laws and policies).
  • Do not adapt RageDance for police, military, or surveillance uses.

Feral Garden Snapshot

RageDance grows like a feral garden: no masters, no gatekeepers, no paywall. It lives in the commons, changes shape across contexts, and belongs to everyone and no one.
